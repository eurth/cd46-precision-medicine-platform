## SCREEN 1 — FOR46 / SAR-CD46 (AstraZeneca / Sanofi) (90 seconds)

[DO: Click or highlight the FOR46 row in the pipeline table. Show trial details panel if available.]

[SAY:]
"FOR46 — the internal study name — is a CD46-targeted antibody-drug conjugate
developed in collaboration with AstraZeneca and originally identified at UCSF.
The trial is registered on ClinicalTrials.gov as NCT04407754.

FOR46 couples a humanised anti-CD46 IgG1 monoclonal antibody to a topoisomerase
I inhibitor pyrrolobenzodiazepine warhead. The mechanism is receptor-mediated
internalisation: after binding to CD46 on the tumour cell surface,
the ADC is internalised, the linker is cleaved in the lysosome,
and the cytotoxic payload kills the CD46-expressing cell.

Phase 1 dose escalation is ongoing in mCRPC. Preliminary results presented
at ASCO 2023 showed a partial response in a heavily pre-treated population.
The target population in the Phase 1 expansion is PSMA-low, CD46-positive mCRPC —
the same patient population we have been discussing throughout this platform demo.

This is particularly relevant: AstraZeneca selected the PSMA-low, CD46-positive
stratification as their expansion cohort criterion — independently validating
the co-biomarker patient selection hypothesis that is built into this platform."

## SCREEN 2 — BC8-CD46 Pretargeted Radioimmunotherapy (Fred Hutchinson) (75 seconds)

[DO: Click or highlight the BC8-CD46 row in the pipeline table.]

[SAY:]
"The second major CD46 clinical programme is at the Fred Hutchinson Cancer Center.
BC8-CD46 is a pretargeted radioimmunotherapy — a fundamentally different delivery
mechanism from an ADC.

In pretargeted RIT, a bispecific antibody molecule is administered first —
one arm binds CD46, the other arm harbours a clearing or docking chemistry.
Hours later, when the antibody has saturated tumour sites and cleared from
normal tissue, a small radiolabelled molecule is administered that locks
onto the pre-positioned antibody.

This two-step approach dramatically reduces off-target radiation exposure
compared to directly labelled antibody — because small molecules clear from
blood and kidneys far faster than full-sized antibodies.

The Fred Hutchinson programme has published proof-of-concept data in
mCRPC cell lines and patient-derived xenografts, with dosimetry showing
favourable tumour-to-normal tissue ratios. Phase 1 registration is anticipated.

Both programmes — FOR46 and BC8-CD46 — independently demonstrate that
CD46 is a clinically tractable radioimmunotherapy target in mCRPC."

## SCREEN 3 — 225Ac vs 177Lu Mechanism Table (75 seconds)

[DO: Point to the mechanism comparison table or visual — alpha vs beta comparison.]

[SAY:]
"Finally — and this is directly relevant to your Actinium programme —
the pipeline page has a direct comparison of the two isotope options
for targeted radionuclide therapy.

Lutetium-177: beta emitter. Path length in tissue: 2 to 3 millimetres.
Particle range: multiple cell diameters. Total mechanism: DNA strand breaks
from beta particle interaction, predominantly single-strand breaks.

Actinium-225: alpha emitter. Path length: 2 to 3 cell diameters — approximately
40 to 100 micrometres. Mechanism: high-linear-energy-transfer alpha particles,
causing primarily double-strand DNA breaks. 20 times more DNA double-strand breaks
per unit absorbed dose compared to beta irradiation.

The clinical implication: alpha emitters are more appropriate for small tumour
clusters, circulating tumour cells, and micrometastatic disease — because the
short path length minimises normal tissue irradiation while the high LET
maximises kill on the targeted cell. Lutetium is more appropriate for
larger, well-vascularised lesions where dose distribution is critical.

For mCRPC with widespread micrometastatic osseous disease —
the CD46-high, PSMA-low patient — 225Ac-CD46 may have a biological
advantage over equivalent lutetium-based targeting."
