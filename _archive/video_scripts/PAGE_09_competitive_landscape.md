## OPENING — Framing Competition Correctly (45 seconds)

[DO: Click 'Competitive Landscape' in the sidebar. Let the page load fully.]

[SAY:]
"The Competitive Landscape page is often misunderstood by first-time viewers —
so let me frame it correctly before we look at the data.

This module does not position CD46 against PSMA as a competitor.
It positions CD46 as the necessary extension of PSMA in a disease space
where PSMA-targeted therapy, despite being the standard of care,
leaves a large and growing patient population unaddressed.

The competitive landscape we are looking at is the clinical trial space —
who is active, what is the trial density around each target,
and where are the clinical white spaces that a CD46 programme can address?"

## SCREEN 1 — Target Comparison KPI Cards (45 seconds)

[DO: Point to the top KPI strip. Read the numbers clearly.]

[SAY:]
"The numbers at the top set the context.

183 — active registered clinical trials for PSMA-targeted therapies on ClinicalTrials.gov.
This includes PSMA diagnostic agents, therapeutic lutetium, actinium conjugates, bispecifics.
PSMA is a mature, heavily invested clinical space with a landmark Phase 3 approval —
lutetium-PSMA-617 in VISION.

14 — active clinical trials for CD46-targeted therapies. ALL of them early phase.
Most are Phase 1 dose-escalation or Phase 1 expansion. No head-to-head PSMA
versus CD46 trial exists. These 14 trials represent the entire registered
clinical footprint of CD46 targeting at this moment.

The ratio: 13-to-1. That is also 13-to-1 years of clinical development lead
that PSMA has over CD46 — which means now is the moment to invest in CD46
clinical data generation, before it reaches the same saturation level."

## SCREEN 2 — Clinical Trial Funnel (90 seconds)

[DO: Point to the funnel visualisation showing 769 → 414 → 345.
     Start at the top of the funnel and work down.]

[SAY:]
"The trial funnel tracks the global clinical landscape from broadest to
most specific for prostate cancer radiopharmaceuticals.

769 — total oncology trials involving radiopharmaceuticals or nuclear medicine
therapy retrieved in the last GENIE-linked data pull.

414 — trials specifically involving prostate cancer patients as the primary
or secondary indication.

345 — active trials — not just registered but currently recruiting, active,
or in follow-up — in prostate cancer with a radioligand or targeted radionuclide
mechanism.

Of those 345, the vast majority — 183 — are PSMA-targeted. CD46 represents 14.
The gap between PSMA and CD46 in the clinical trial space is entirely explained
by the 8-to-10 year head start PSMA had in the academic discovery pipeline.

This is the green field. This is where a CD46 programme sits."

## SCREEN 3 — Side-by-Side Target Comparison (75 seconds)

[DO: Point to the target comparison table or bar chart showing PSMA vs CD46 attributes:
     expression frequency, clinical trial count, approved therapies, patient fraction addressed, etc.]

[SAY:]
"The target comparison table brings together the key attributes across the
two most clinically relevant prostate radiopharmaceutical targets.

PSMA expression: 80 to 90% of primary prostate cancer, declining with
advanced disease, absent in up to 35% of mCRPC post-treatment.
CD46 expression: low in normal prostate, significantly upregulated in
CRPC and mCRPC, inversely correlated with PSMA.

Approved therapies: PSMA — lutetium-PSMA-617, FDA approved 2022.
CD46 — none. Zero. The first approval is still to be earned.

This tells you two things simultaneously. First, the risk is higher for CD46
because there is no approval yet defining the pathway. Second, the value capture
opportunity is entirely open — no one has defined the standard of care.

The clinical complementarity is the strategic message:
you use PSMA to treat the PSMA-positive, treatment-naive mCRPC population.
You use CD46 when PSMA expression has declined. Combined, you extend the
nuclear medicine treatment continuum across the full disease arc."
