## OPENING — Why a Multi-Marker Approach (45 seconds)

[DO: Click 'Biomarker Panel' in the sidebar. Wait for page to load.]

[SAY:]
"The Biomarker Panel module addresses one of the central strategic questions
in radiopharmaceutical development: can we combine CD46 with existing
biomarkers — particularly PSMA — to define the patient population that would
benefit from CD46-targeted therapy, and to identify which patients have
already exhausted PSMA targeting?

The clinical hypothesis is straightforward. PSMA and CD46 are inversely
correlated in metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer. As disease
progresses under androgen receptor pathway inhibition or after PSMA-targeted
therapy, PSMA expression declines and CD46 expression rises.
A dual-biomarker panel — not just PSMA, not just CD46 — gives you coverage
across the full treatment-resistant disease spectrum."

## SCREEN 1 — KPI Metric Strip (45 seconds)

[DO: Point to the 5 summary metric cards at the top of the page.]

[SAY:]
"Five at-a-glance metrics for the mCRPC biomarker landscape:

43% — Percentage of mCRPC patients in the SU2C cohort with a named CD46-related
molecular alteration — AR, PTEN, or TP53 — that mechanistically connects to
CD46 upregulation.

444 — The SU2C mCRPC cohort size. West Coast Dream Team dataset, deeply sequenced,
treatment history annotated. Not a synthetic dataset.

Approximately 35% — Proportion of mCRPC patients estimated to be PSMA-low
based on diagnostic scan data from published Phase 3 PSMA-RLT trials.
These are the patients who cannot benefit from lutetium-PSMA but may be
CD46-high.

Greater than 85% — Combined coverage: PSMA alone captures approximately 65%.
Add CD46. The intersection of PSMA-addressed patients plus CD46-addressed
patients gives you over 85% of the mCRPC patient population with at least
one molecularly targeted treatment option."

## SCREEN 2 — Co-targeting Coverage Chart (90 seconds)

[DO: Point to the stacked bar or Venn diagram showing PSMA vs CD46 coverage.
     If a 2D scatter or complementarity chart is shown, point to it directly.]

[SAY:]
"This visualisation shows the clinical case for CD46 as a complementary target
to PSMA — not a competing one. The framework presented here should inform
how CD46 is positioned in any grant proposal or regulatory conversation.

The PSMA-low, CD46-high quadrant is the priority. These patients have no
approved second-line nuclear medicine option post-PSMA therapy. This is
the addressable patient population for a first-in-class 225Ac CD46 programme.

The PSMA-high, CD46-low quadrant remains the domain of current lutetium
PSMA-617 therapy. The PSMA-high, CD46-high quadrant is the dual-positive patient —
potentially eligible for combination or sequential RLT strategies.

The co-targeting framework does not require CD46 to replace PSMA.
It requires CD46 to expand the reach of nuclear medicine into the population
PSMA misses."

## SCREEN 3 — GENIE 8-Segment Landscape (90 seconds)

[DO: Point to the 8-segment grouped bar chart by cancer type showing
     AR/PTEN/TP53 co-alteration frequency in the prostate cohort.]

[SAY:]
"You saw this data on Page 2 in the Patient Selection module. On the Biomarker
Panel page, we are now showing the same 8-segment model as a biomarker
stratification framework rather than a patient selection framework.

Each segment represents a molecularly defined patient group with a distinct
signal profile and a distinct treatment-resistance mechanism:
Triple-hit patients — AR, PTEN, TP53 — have the highest expected CD46 surface
expression based on complement pathway co-regulation studies.
PTEN-only and AR-only patients have intermediate expected expression levels.
The no-alteration group — 56% of patients — would require direct IHC or
RNA measurement to confirm CD46 status before enrolment.

This model is directly usable as a patient stratification framework for a
biomarker-stratified screening protocol in a Phase 1 expansion cohort."
